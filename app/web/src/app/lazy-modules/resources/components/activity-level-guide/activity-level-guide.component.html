How Do I Choose My Activity Level?
◈ The activity level you choose will have a major impact on your initial TDEE estimate. Metabolism Equivalents (METs) were used to develop the standard activity level factors. One MET is defined as the oxygen consumed while sitting at rest. The most scientific way to find your physical activity level is to list out all your activities in a day, the time you spent doing them in minutes, and add up the equivalent METs.
◈ This is time-consuming and not practical for most people. Instead, we can choose from general levels. These are suggested in the National Dietary Reference Intakes. Listed below are simplified descriptions divided into FOUR categories that we and others use.
◈ If you are reading this resource because you have concerns about which activity level you picked when signing up, don't worry too much. Our algorithm relies on the activity level you chose just to make your initial TDEE estimation. As our algorithm learns from your data, it will become closer and closer to your true TDEE over time. Choose the category that corresponds to a lower level of activity if you are unsure between two categories.
Sedentary
People who don't exercise or play sports or work in sedentary jobs use this. An example would be a computer programmer who works in a small office and drives to work. *We recommend this option if you are unsure*
Lightly Active
This is used for people with sedentary jobs that exercise 2 - 3 times per week. An example would be an office clerk who goes to a circuit training class three times per week.
Active
This is used for those who have moderately active jobs and exercise 3 - 4 times per week. An example would be a construction worker who lifts heavy objects and is on his/her feet all day, and also exercises after work.
Very Active
This is used for those who have highly active jobs, and exercise 4 - 6 times per week. Professional athletes (soccer, football, basketball, etc.,) fall into this category.
